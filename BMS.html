Battery Management System

A system the redistributes energy from the stronger cells to the weaker cells in a battery pack in order to maximize the capacity avaiable to the load at its operating voltage.

Problem

Over time, in a battery pack, individual cells begin to degrade, this leads to the battery pack voltage decreasing prematurely. Even though there is still energy stored in the other "stonger" battery cells, the weak one acts as a bottle neck. The picture to the left shows the dissipation curves of 4 different lithium iron phosphate battery cells. Cell number 4 is damaged and the voltage drops off sooner than the others. This means the system will only last until this cell loses its charge, significantly reducing the state of charge of the battery pack. The weak cells act as a “weak point” and reduces the ability of the pack to hold a charge. Once the voltage of weak cell drops, the entire pack voltage will drop below the loads minimum operating point. In most applications, this causes the load to stop operating correctly.

How it works

The goal of this system is to prolong the life time of battery packs. Our system transfers energy from the battery pack to any weak cell. The figure to the left shows a pack to cell active battery balancing topology.

The overall hardware design consists of a step-down DC/DC converter that outputs 5V. The 5V supplies power to the entire board and also charges the weak cell. A Hall effect current sensor, along with an analog to digital converter sends data to the microcontroller which is fed into the charge estimation algorithm. The function of the microcontroller is to determine which of the cells are degraded and then charges using a state of charge estimation algorithm. Once the weak cell is detected, the switching circuitry will charge this cell.
